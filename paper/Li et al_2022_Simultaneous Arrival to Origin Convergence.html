Simultaneous-Arrival-to-Origin Convergence: Sliding-Mode Control through the Norm-Normalized Sign Function

Dongyu Li, Shuzhi Sam Ge, and Tong Heng Lee
Abstract-In this note, simultaneous-arrival-to-origin (SATO) convergence is defined - all state elements arriving at the origin at the same time. Accordingly, a relevant sufficient condition is proposed for SATO convergence. Based on this formulation of SATO convergence, the classical and norm-normalized (NN) sign functions are revisited. Their differences are studied with applications in sliding-mode control design. Both functions (expectedly when properly invoked) contribute to system stability, while the NN sign function enables the system to accomplish SATO convergence. This finding shows the distinctive merit of the NN sign function in achieving more than finite-time stability for a sliding-mode control system. Extensions to the scenario with a networked system are studied, where using the NN sign function, the networked system (now with the SATO convergence property) drives all the agents to reach consensus simultaneously. Additionally, for double integrator systems and Euler-Lagrange systems, singularity-free sliding mode control laws are designed and demonstrated to achieve SATO convergence.
Index Terms-Norm-normalized sign functions, simultaneous-arrival-to-origin convergence.

I. INTRODUCTION

F INITE-time control emerges to meet the high-precision and fast-response performance required by progressive control tasks. finite-time stabilization (as the notion implies) offers the closed-loop system convergence in finite time [1]; under which, the situation is such that before a certain time instant, all the state elements reach the origin. Its wide applications include satellite stabilization [2], manipulator tracking [3], multi-agent consensus [4], state estimation [5], control under input constraints [6], among others. For some classes of commonly encountered practical systems, this characteristic of finite-time convergence alone is not adequate for some tasks, e.g., for a robotic hand, in certain situations where its joints are to simultaneously arrive at target angles (say, as a specific requirement for some particular task). In this note, we further investigate a new finite-time convergence formulation - simultaneous-arrival-to-origin (SATO) convergence, where at the same time, all the state elements are driven to the origin.
To address this problem, we first revisit sign functions well-utilized in control design and data processing [7]-[14]. Moreover, the sign functions integrated in sliding-mode control
make the control system robust to matched disturbances [15], [16]. We are interested in focusing beyond just the usual sign functions; thus studying two kinds of sign functions - the classical sign function and the norm-normalized (NN) sign function, as well as how these functions affect the related control system outcomes (this latter NN sign function was previously utilized by the co-authors in [17], although not so-named there). The NN sign function incorporates an input vector and its norm by normalization computation, which also serves as a unit direction vector. We call it the norm-normalized sign function because it holds many properties similar to those of a classical sign function, e.g., they both show the general direction of an input vector. In the literature, similar functions are also called as the unit vector [18] (Chapter 3.6, The Unit Vector Approach), the unit controller [19] (Chapter 3.5, Unit Control), the scaled unit vector [20], the vector-valued function [21], to name a few. As mentioned, the appropriate utilization of the two sign functions have been earlier presented in [17]; but there, while some examples of effectiveness were shown, their influence on a control system in terms of the convergence performance were not further deeply explored nor developed. Note that although the NN sign function had been likewise studied in sliding-mode control design for multi-input-multi-output systems [18]-[22] to construct the sliding-mode surface, the relation between the NN sign function and SATO convergence has not been fully explored in these efforts. In addition, although the result in [21] is proposed to achieve finite-time stabilization, SATO convergence had (in fact) also been simultaneously achieved (albeit without further detailed investigation and proof, as this aspect was likely not a focus of the work there). This note presents that two types of sign functions attain different properties in the following sense: (i) in terms of control design, both functions (expectedly when properly invoked) are capable of designing sliding-mode controllers to achieve finite-time convergence; and (ii) the NN sign function additionally (as distinctive merit) provides SATO convergence to the closed-loop system.
In this note, we formally propose a definition of SATO convergence - i.e., a system achieves SATO convergence if at the same time, all the elements of the closed-loop state vector reach the equilibrium, which can be treated as a specific and unique type of the usual established finite-time convergence notion. Next, in the detailed developments below, we propose a relevant sufficient condition for SATO convergence. Based on this sufficient condition (and also the new attainments now made possible with the NN sign function), we propose sliding-mode control laws for an appropriate single system and for multi-agent systems, that attain for them the noteworthy outcome of SATO convergence; and applicable extensions to second-order systems are also discussed. Note that for the multi-agent consensus problem presented, it is rigorously established and guaranteed that all the agents achieve consensus at the same time, (i.e., SATO consensus). Moreover, this approach is of practical importance, such as in the following illustrative instances: (i) it is essential for a 6-DOF on-orbit service satellite to achieve SATO convergence in the short-range relative motion during rendezvous and docking; and likewise (ii) in light of various commonly encountered consensus-based multi-agent missions, SATO convergence offers crucial advantages to the whole operation [23], e.g., in cooperative attack, transport, and formation missions [24]. Additionally and advantageously, from the related theoretical analysis, we further find that the SATO convergence property can enhance the overall system performance by smoothing the system output and reducing energy consumption. This interesting property attains for the control system a more desirable outcome in practice (e.g.,in terms of shortening the travel length and saving the fuel).

A. Simultaneous-Arrival-to-Origin Convergence

We formally define the SATO convergence property.
Definition 1: The solution of the system (9) holds the simultaneous-arrival-to-origin (SATO) convergence property if for all x0 ,there exists a time instant T(x0)<∞ ,such that all the state elements whose initial value xi(0)≠0 converge to the origin at the same time,i.e., ∀xi with xi(0)≠0 ,we have xi(t)=0 ,for all t≥T ,while xi(t)≠0 ,for all t<T ; and all the state elements whose initial value xi(0)=0 stay at the origin,i.e., ∀xi with xi(0)=0 ,we have xi(t)=0 ,for all t≥0 .
In the sequel, to facilitate a proper sufficient condition to achieve SATO convergence, we introduce the concept of Ratio Persistence.
Definition 2: The state x of the system (9) is ratio persistent if for x≠0 ,we have
(11)x∥x∥=ζf(x)∥f(x)∥
where ζ can be either 1 or -1 .
"Ratio persistence" above is one which is articulated to characterize a class of system whose ratio of each pair of state elements is a constant (a general condition also satisfied in [18]-[21]). In addition, the classical proportional control also enables a system state to be ratio persistent, which is the simplest version of a ratio persistent system. It is also noteworthy that there is a close connection between ratio persistence and the NN sign function signn .
We are now ready to present the fundamental Lyapunov result of SATO convergence based on ratio persistence and finite-time stability, which will be applied throughout the paper.

Due to the continuity of the system (9),we have xj(Ti)=0 , and the contradiction occurs since xj reaches zero at Tj not at Ti . Therefore,all the state elements reach the equilibrium simultaneously, and the closed-loop system achieves SATO convergence, which concludes the proof.
Remark 1: Theorem 1 reveals that, finite-time convergence and ratio persistence contribute to SATO convergence. Further, in the later discussion, we will show that the NN sign function signn is essential in the achievement of ratio persistence.
In fact, to achieve SATO convergence, it is not necessarily to have ratio "persistence". If the ratio of each pair of the system state elements is not constant but a bounded non-zero variable, we will get a more relaxed sufficient condition for SATO convergence. For example, we can define Ratio Restriction as: the closed-loop state x of the system (9) is ratio restricted if for x≠0 ,we have that the ratio of each pair of the state elements rij(t)=xi(t)/xj(t) exits. The function rij(t) is constrained as Rm≤‖rij(t)‖≤RM ,where Rm and RM can be any positive constants.
Then, we can straightforwardly get a result that the equilibrium of the system (9) achieve SATO convergence, if the following conditions hold:
i. the system (9) is finite-time stable;
ii. The state x is ratio restricted.
The proof is similar to that of Theorem 1, which can be given by direct contradiction.
Despite the more generalized conditions of SATO convergence when utilizing the concept of ratio restriction, in most cases, we are fond of building a SATO controller with the ratio persistence property (rather than the ratio restriction property). The main reason is that a ratio-persistent system trajectory is the shortest path in 2- and 3-dimensional Euclidean space. In addition, if the ratio restriction property is properly utilized to achieve SATO convergence, it is still complicated to analyze the system performance before the convergence since the ratio constraints Rm and RM could be arbitrary small/large. In this book, we thus naturally focus on the control design based on ratio persistence.
In the following subsections, for a comprehensive comparison of SATO convergence and finite-time convergence, both the classical and NN sign functions are applied to construct sliding-mode control laws in several important cases: (i) utilize signc and signn to design a finite-time controller and a SATO control law for first-order systems, respectively; (ii) introduce sigc and sign to improve the control design and to avoid the discontinuity; (iii) extend the SATO convergence property to multi-agent consensus problems; and (iv) for second-order systems, propose sliding-mode controllers to achieve SATO convergence and avoid the singularity problem.

IV. CONCLUSION

The SATO (simultaneous-arrival-to-origin) control problem has been defined in this note, and the appropriate and relevant sufficient condition has been addressed for this SATO convergence formulation. Several sliding-mode controllers using both the classical and norm-normalized (NN) sign functions have been proposed. It is noted that both of these sign functions (expectedly when properly invoked) stabilize a system, while the latter one additionally enables the potential to obtain SATO convergence. Extensions to networked systems have also been provided, and it is shown that it is possible (with the attainments now made possible with the NN sign function) to drive all the agents to achieve consensus (or consensus-based formation) at the same time. In addition, singularity-free sliding-mode controllers (which avoids additional discontinuities in the control law) have been suitably proposed to achieve SATO convergence.
As the NN sign function is widely utilized in a wide range of research and industry fields, it endows this approach with possibly rather broad applicability and further extends the possible scope of this contribution. Nevertheless, it is acknowledged that various important directions and pertinent issues remain as open problems, e.g., how to realize SATO convergence in uncertain environments.
